Youth unemployment in Europe: Far from being history only

When the economic crisis hit Europe in 2008, pictures of protesting unemployed young people in Spain and Greece became iconic symbols of the crisis. Data analysis shows how the situation got even worse after that.

From the offset of the crisis in 2008, the situation got even worse in countries like Greece, Spain or Croatia and reached highest rates in 2013. In the following years, situation improved slightly. Still, across Europe 5 million young people under 25 years old are currently unemployed. That equals every fifth young European.

However the situation varies widely across the continent: Coutries iconic for the crisis are still worse off than Western European countries for example.

In fact: Whereas in Germany many traineeship places remain empty due to a lack of qualified or interested trainees, thousands of young Spanish people are desperately seeking a job. To just shuttle them to Europe is neither a solution, experts say. Because in the long run, Spain's economy will need skilled workers.

But if the situation for them won't change, experts fear, they might become a lost generation: hard to reintegrate into work force, not knowing about structured work-days. Estimates put the production loss at 153 billion euros per year — which is in the long run much more expensive then channeling money for immediate creation of traineeship places.

At the beginning of December 2016 the European Union announced to invest additional 500 million euros in the Youth Employment Initiative that's meant to guarantee each European under 25 years old to find a job within four month after leaving school.

Experts estimate though that it would take up to 75 billion euros to put all 5 million unemployed young Europeans into labour.

Studies highlight, how national economy and labour market structure are crucial to implementing sustainable job initiatives. Additionally, critics say that it's too difficult for countries to obtain money from the fund — which is one of the reason why the situation is improving only slowly.
